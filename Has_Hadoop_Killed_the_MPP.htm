Has Hadoop Killed the MPP?

Hadoop with its HDFS (Highly Distributed File System) and its plethora of tools including SPARK, PIG, Hive, Ranger, Knox etc. have in recent months come into an ever increasing conflict with the current and long established vendors in a very similar area of MPP (Massively Parallel Processing).

The traditionally well-established vendors of Teradata, Netezza, Vertica and Greenplum have over the last 20+ years serviced an extremely lucrative field, applying highly optimised technology to provide the capability of very fast reads and high performing JOINS. This performance has been provided in a number of ways including the configuration of hardware to boost a variety of specific use cases.

Even in relatively recent times much has been talked about the Hadoop HDFS ecosystem being complementary to the MPP system, stating that HDFS was primarily built for the fast batch loading of data but running complex joins in a predominantly Map Reduce system takes too long, and that while MPP systems generally take more pre-processing time to get data into the system, once there the performance benefits are massive.

This is all fine, however as SPARK and other Hadoop based tools mature and as the commodity hardware that this echo system is binded to, improves exponentially, the GAP between these traditional vendors, and their capability to be agile and move their complex optimisations to the next level, is steadily getting harder.

If you then look at the how this MPP software is built, then we see a further problem for these Traditional vendors of Netezza and Teradata software. This proprietary software is a closely guarded secret and with a relatively few people able to work at this level within these vendors constraints vs the open software approach where a mass army of developers are focused on a modern and sexy method of providing ever growing performance, it just seems a matter of time before these tech giants will change forever.

So has Hadoop killed MPP? Not yet, but the writing is definitely on the wall.
